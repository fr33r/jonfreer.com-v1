News story about our accomplishment on making it to Tier One Nationals.

Up to this point in my hockey life, I had worked my way up from recreational league hockey from when I was 12 or so, to AA travel hockey. I continuously pushed myself to be the best I could be, and I played as much hockey as I possibly could.

The AAA Columbus Blue Jackets is the only AAA hockey organization in Columbus. AAA hockey in Ohio is the highest intensity level that a player can reach, as well as the highest compitition. Making it to this level of play demonstrated that you had a high potential to move on to play juniors and/or college hockey.

After trying out several years and not being chosen, I entered Nationwide Arena in 2008 with ever high focus and intensity for the tryouts. These tryouts were always nerve wrecking for me because at this level of play, a lot of politics come into the picture. It was basically a given that members of the team the previous year had a pass to be on the team next year, so there were always limited spots to compete for.

After a great tryout, I beat out thirteen other goaltenders for the one spot available on the team. My hockey dreams had finally come true, and I could say that I had become one of the elite hockey players in Columbus.

We made our way around the country; anywhere from Blaine, Minnesota to Plano, Texas. We played the best teams in the country and even beat some of them. I was shown everything that hockey was in the United States.

I learned a lot this year. A lot about myself. A lot about other people. A lot about coaches. A lot about handling situations. I thought that when I had made the team that the hardest part was over. I didn't think about the hardships that came along with this level of play.

Remember, it was my first year in the organization. I was practically an outsider. The team I had made was the U16 AAA Blue Jackets, a merge between the 1992 and 1993 birth years. Out of the two groups, I didn't really fit into either since they had formed based on their respective teams the year(s) before.

However, I had a good start to the season. I played relatively well for never playing at the intensity before, and had some moments where I impressed my teammates, coaches, and those who watched me.

Near the middle of the season, my coaches made a decision to alter the equal time split between me and the other goaltender on the team. They made sure that I knew that it was not due to my performance, and I remember at times that they actually praised my performance before following with another statement that I would not be playing the next game.

I felt my drive dip for the first time in my life. I had worked so hard to be there, perfected many areas of my play that the coaches had set out for me to improve, and always gave every game my all.

I started to realize why this was happening and the coaches were right, it had nothing to do with my play. Politics had its way of twisting things up, and unfortunately I was the only one impacted.

Despite this, I still made my way between the pipes fairly often and proved that I was still worthy of the spot. I had parents of other teams come up to me and tell me how great I did. I had parents of our team asking me why things were the way they were and I couldn't really explain it.

Our team made our way to Tier One Nationals in Saint Louis, Missouri after winning Regionals. This was the first time in orginization history and it was incredible to have been a part of that. Our accomplishment was even honored during the National Anthem at a Blue Jackets game, with all of us lined up on the blue line.

Although, I didn't get to play in Nationals as much as I would have wanted, I still look at it as an amazing event in my life. To say that we were one of sixteen teams in the United States to compete in Nationals is truly amazing, and someting few can say.

Many of the players on my team went on to be successful. Two of them have even gone on to be drafted to the NHL.

The most important take away from this experience was actually me. A bit odd to say I know, but it was incredible how far I had come in one year. My agility on the ice was honed, my sight for the puck as well as situations unfolding on the ice were sharp, and my competitiveness was at an all time high. I was the best I could be. I am still proud of that.
